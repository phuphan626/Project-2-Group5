Comments and Considerations

A story written in data

Baseball has always been a numbers game. Upon visualizing 100 years of baseball data we can identify ways the game has changed, and ways it's stayed the same. There are a million reasons for these changes or consistencies, ranging from new rules and technologies, to new ballparks and world wars. The best way to try and understand the changes in the game of baseball is to analyze the visualizations and compare against any relevant factors in the time period.

We chose these stats because we wanted to have representation on both sides of the ball, pitching and batting. Our initial data exploration consisted of identifying obvious trends visible to the naked eye, reflected in the "Strikeouts per 9 Innings" and "Games Finished" stats. The consistent uptrend in the values of these statistics can be traced back to baseball's early days, and the natural evolution of the game itself. In the early 1920's the rules governing the actual baseball itself were changed. The ball was designed to be hit harder and further, and pitchers weren't allowed to smudge dirt and spit on the ball to hinder its flight. According to the data, the finesse required to throw quality pitches clearly took longer to develop on a league-wide basis than the concentration required for a batter to put the ball in play. This is reflected by the consistent "Runs per Game" and "Earned Run Average" stats compared to the steady uptrend in "Strikeouts per 9 Innings".

The consistent uptrend in "Games Finished" as reflected in the radial pitching graph can be attributed to the incorporation of relief pitchers and eventually closing pitchers. In the early days of baseball, a player could only be substituted in the instance of sickness or injury. Ineffective pitchers would often switch positions with someone else on the field. In the 1920's we began to see the rise of a new position: the relief pitcher. Upon analyzing the data we can see the gradual rise of the relief pitcher and closing pitcher. In 1920 around 50% of games were finished by a relief pitcher as opposed to 99% of games in 2019.

The changes in batting statistics over the past century can be attributed to shifts in tactics and style of play. Upon examining the radial batting graph we can clearly see the amount of doubles per game is relatively constant. Looking at triples and stolen bases we begin to get a better picture of how greatly the style of play has changed in 100 years of baseball. When we take doubles out of the equation, we are able to get a better look at the interplaying relationship between the triple and the stolen bag. Looking at triples and stolen bases together we can see that their rates go hand in hand. From the 1920's to the mid-1940's the triple is king, indicating a style of play where speed and extra base hits are prioritized. Around the middle of the century the triple rate falls off drastically and the stolen base rate increases rapidly, indicating a shift from riskier baserunning to a style of play called "Small Ball" where tactical baserunning and finesse hitting is employed to advance baserunners. This indicates a major shift in the way the game was played and enjoyed.

Upon examining the line graph for homeruns per game we can see an almost cyclical uptrend. This gives us an opportunity to try and address why these crests and troughs occur by cross-referencing the data with historical developments, baseball-related or otherwise. One can feel confident attributing the decrease in the home run rate from .64 per game in 1940 to .37 per game in 1943 to the over 500 major leaguers that served in the military during World War II with sluggers Joe DiMaggio, Ted Williams and Stan Musial among them.

The final statistic we will explore is the "Batting Average". The graph appears to be relatively constant but there is a steady downtrend in the league batting average over the past century. When we couple this with the meteoric rise in "Stikeouts per 9 Innings" stat, it's not hard to understand why.
